Circe's Island: Aeaea

A lush island with "deep... wooded glens" (Homer Book 10), where one can find docile animals native to the land and hear the nymph Circe "singing, lifting her spellbinding voice as she glides back and forth at her great immortal loom" (Homer Book 10). A clear river runs through the land, where stags big enough to feed even the great Odysseus and his crewmates stop to drink and graze. While a wonderful vacation spot, there are unfortunately no lodgings currently available unless one would like to live in a pigpen - literally. Contact Circe for more details.

Sparta

Sparta is a land revered for its soldiers. Through extensive training, Spartans are among the best in land-based warfare. The great king Menelaus has resided in his palace here, where Prince Telemachus "feasted within the grand, high-roofed palace, all the kin and clansmen of Menelaus in his glory, reveling warmly here as in their midst an inspired bard sang out and struck his lyre- and through them a pair of tumblers dashed and sprang, whirling in leaping handsprings, leading on the dance." (Homer Book 4).

Ithaca

Ithaca is Odysseus' homeland. Ithaca is a grand island with a mountain in its wake. It is home to the palace of Odysseus, where he will eventually return to. Additionally, Ithaca is home to Odysseus' son, Telemachus, and his wife Penelope. There, one can find many shepherds and herders, along with their flocks of sheep and other domestic animals. Unfortunately, suitors run rampant in Odysseus' halls, defacing and destroying of his palace, but they will soon be driven out.

The Land of the Cyclopes

The Land of the Cyclopes is a wonderful island with rams and sheep galore, tended to by the Cyclopes. These cyclopes inhabit the land and while their caves may be plentiful in food, be wary of the them. These brutes carry clubs that are "big enough to be the mast of a pitch-black ship with her twenty oars, a freighter broad in the beam that plows through miles of sea— so long, so thick..." (Homer Book 9). If they catch you enjoying their property, you do not want to be in the way of their club. Nevertheless, this island is a beautiful sight to see and while unkempt by the cyclopes, displays great potential and fertility. Odysseus himself even said that through the right care, the land could have "no mean spot, it could bear you any crop you like in season. The water-meadows along the low foaming shore run soft and moist, and your vines would never flag. The land’s clear for plowing. Harvest on harvest, a man could reap a healthy stand of grain— the subsoil is dark and rich" (Homer Book 9).
